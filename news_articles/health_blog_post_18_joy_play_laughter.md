# The Afternoon I Learned to Play Again: On Joy, Laughter, and the Forgotten Art of Silliness

I am a person who used to make things unnecessarily difficult. Not problems—problems I could handle. I mean joy. I made joy difficult. Earned, I believed, through enough productivity. Appropriate to certain contexts but suspicious when unwarranted. The kind of thing you experience when circumstances justify it, not because the afternoon is beautiful and you happen to have a frisbee.

I realized this at a company team-building event—an afternoon in a park that I had attended with the resigned competence of someone who would rather be catching up on email. We were doing some kind of collaborative activity involving rubber balls, and at some point, inexplicably, I threw a rubber ball at my colleague James and he failed spectacularly to catch it, falling dramatically backward onto the grass in a way that was objectively undignified and absolutely hilarious. I laughed—really laughed, from somewhere unguarded and automatic—and then noticed that I couldn't remember the last time I had laughed like that. Not the polite, conversational chuckling of professional social interaction. Actual laughter. The kind that makes you catch your breath.

On the drive home I did a rough mental calculation and estimated it had been over a year.

## What adults lose when they forget how to play

The psychiatrist Stuart Brown spent three decades studying play—as a researcher, as a clinician who worked with violent criminals tracing their histories backward, and as the founder of the National Institute for Play. What he found, across thousands of "play histories" from people across the lifespan, was this: the people who had the richest and most joyful lives, the most resilient and creative and satisfying adult lives, were consistently those who had maintained some form of play into adulthood. And the people who had the most diminished lives—highest rates of depression, most rigid and brittle in the face of change—were those for whom play had been extinguished, either by circumstance, by criticism, or by the cultural messaging that seriousness equals maturity.

Play, Brown argues, is not merely entertainment. It is a primary biological drive—as fundamental to our development and functioning as sleep—and its absence produces measurable deficits in creativity, adaptability, cognitive flexibility, and emotional resilience. Animal research supports this: young mammals deprived of play opportunities develop abnormal stress responses, reduced social competence, and impaired problem-solving—deficits that persist into adulthood even when the deprivation was temporary.

The brain scientist Jaak Panksepp identified PLAY as one of seven primary emotional operating systems in mammalian brains—a distinct neural circuit with its own characteristic neurotransmitters and hormones, whose function is not merely to provide pleasure but to support the development of social intelligence, creativity, and emotional flexibility. Play, in Panksepp's framework, is not what humans do when they have nothing serious to do. It is how the mammalian brain learns to be human.

## The neuroscience of laughter and what it costs you to suppress it

Laughter—the most social and contagious expression of play—has its own robust body of research, and the findings are startling.

Dr. Lee Berk at Loma Linda University has spent decades studying the physiological effects of mirthful laughter. His findings: laughter reduces serum levels of cortisol (the primary stress hormone) and epinephrine (adrenaline), decreases pro-inflammatory markers, increases natural killer cell activity (a key measure of immune function), releases endorphins and dopamine, and activates the parasympathetic nervous system—the "rest and digest" branch that counteracts the fight-or-flight response. A single episode of genuine laughter, Berk's research shows, produces physiological changes comparable to moderate exercise, with measurable benefits persisting for up to 24 hours afterward.

Research by Dr. Sophie Scott at University College London using neuroimaging shows that the sounds of genuine laughter automatically activate the premotor cortex of listeners—the region preparing the muscles of the face to smile—before any conscious processing occurs. Laughter is literally contagious at the neural level: we are wired to catch each other's joy.

The implications run in both directions. When we suppress play and laughter—when we choose dignified seriousness over silliness, maintain the mask of professional gravity in every context—we are not merely forgoing pleasure. We are depriving ourselves of a biological regulatory system that our nervous systems require to function well. The stoic executive is not tougher. They are simply more cortisol-soaked, more inflamed, and more isolated from the social bonding that laughter creates.

## The frisbee I bought on the way home

I stopped at a sporting goods store on my drive home from the team-building event. I bought a frisbee—a neon yellow one with a satisfying flexibility. I am not sure exactly why; impulse, perhaps, or the memory of throwing things for the pure pleasure of throwing them when I was eight years old.

That weekend I went to the park with my frisbee and my partner, who was bewildered and delighted in roughly equal measure. We threw it for an hour. I dropped it repeatedly and repeatedly chased it, which is undignified and excellent. We were, by any measure, being silly.

What I noticed: my body felt different afterward. Lighter, somehow—not tired as after exercise but loosened, as though something that had been subtly clenched had been given permission to release. I slept that night deeply and woke up, with some surprise, happy.

## Play as a practice of self-permission

The greater challenge, I found, was not finding the frisbee. It was granting myself permission to use it.

Adults who struggle with play—and there are more of us than the self-help literature acknowledges—often experience a specific internal blockade: the sense that unstructured, purposeless, silly activity is indulgent, wasteful, or evidence of some failure to take life seriously enough. This is the psychology researcher Brené Brown calls "foreboding joy"—the tendency to preemptively armor against happiness rather than allowing it fully, because vulnerability is uncomfortable and joy makes us feel exposed.

The antidote, Brown's research suggests, is not to logic your way past the discomfort but to practice what she calls "gratitude in the moment of joy"—deliberately noticing and naming the joy as you're experiencing it, softening toward it rather than armoring against it. And to practice it regularly, in small ways, until the neural pathways of permission become more familiar than those of suppression.

I have, over the past two years, become a more playful person than I was. Not in every context—there are still meetings I take seriously and problems I approach with appropriate gravity. But I have learned to locate, in most days, a small pocket of silliness. A game. A joke that requires actual commitment. A willingness to look faintly ridiculous and survive it. A conversation that goes nowhere productive and leaves everyone feeling better.

My colleague James and I now have a standing Thursday lunch. We almost never discuss work. We tell each other stories, complain cheerfully about minor irritants, laugh with the specific abandon of people who have decided, together, that life is permitted to be funny.

Research on friendship consistently shows that shared laughter—more than shared history, shared values, or shared experiences—is the primary bond in the friendships that people rate as most important and sustaining. Laughter is not what we do after the serious business of relationship. It is, often, the relationship itself.

## The permission you are waiting for

If you are waiting for life to earn its joy—for circumstances to justify the silliness, for enough serious productivity to license an afternoon of play—you will wait a long time and miss most of the days.

The invitation here is not to be less serious about what matters. It is to understand that joy, play, and laughter are not the absence of seriousness. They are the fuel that makes sustained engagement with serious things possible—the biological regulator, the social glue, the nervous system cleanser, the reminder that being human is, among all its other demanding qualities, occasionally an absolute delight.

You are allowed the frisbee. You always were.
