# The Shape of What Remains: Learning to Live with Loss and Change

My mother died on a Tuesday in March, while I was in the middle of a work meeting I would not be able to recall a single detail of afterward. The call came from my sister. I stepped into a stairwell and sat down on a concrete step with my phone in my hand, and the world divided itself into before and after with the clean finality of a cut.

I want to tell you that I handled it well. I did not. I handled it the way most people handle the death of the person who has been the emotional center of their universe since before they had words for such things: chaotically, inconsistently, with long periods of numbness punctuated by ambushes of grief at entirely inconvenient moments—at the grocery store, in traffic, while brushing my teeth.

What I want to tell you about, though, is not the grief itself—that has been written about with more wisdom and beauty than I can add to. What I want to tell you about is what came after the grief, and what it taught me about the nature of change, impermanence, and the strange, counterintuitive relationship between loss and aliveness.

## The thing nobody told me about grief

Six months after my mother died, I had expected—vaguely, without articulating it—to be "over it." Our culture has an uncomfortable relationship with grief. We offer two weeks of condolences and then, implicitly, expect people to return to functional productivity. We speak of "closure" as though grief has a finish line. We use the word "recovery" in ways that suggest returning to the person we were before.

But grief researchers have spent decades documenting something more complex and ultimately more hopeful. Dr. George Bonanno at Columbia University, whose longitudinal research has tracked bereavement across thousands of subjects, found that human beings are remarkably resilient in the face of loss—more so than popular narratives of grief suggest—and that the majority of people find, within a year or two, not just adaptation but genuine growth from major life losses.

This is not the same as saying loss doesn't matter or doesn't hurt. It is saying that the human capacity to metabolize suffering and find new meaning in its aftermath is one of the most remarkable features of our species. Psychologists Richard Tedeschi and Lawrence Calhoun coined the term "post-traumatic growth" to describe the positive psychological changes—increased personal strength, deepened relationships, expanded perspective, heightened appreciation for life, and spiritual/existential growth—that many people report following major loss or adversity. Their research, subsequently replicated across hundreds of studies, found that post-traumatic growth occurs not despite suffering but through it—that the wrestling with loss, the forced confrontation with what matters, is itself the mechanism of transformation.

## What my mother's death taught me about impermanence

The Buddhist concept of *anicca*—impermanence, the teaching that all phenomena are transient and that clinging to permanence is the root of suffering—had always struck me as philosophically interesting but emotionally distant. Losing my mother made it personal.

Everything I had unconsciously counted on—her voice on the phone on Sunday afternoons, her particular way of asking if I was eating properly, the knowledge that she was there—was simply gone. And with her absence came a confrontation with every other impermanence I had been managing not to notice: the impermanence of health, of time, of the people I loved, of the version of myself I currently inhabited.

This confrontation was, initially, terrifying. And then, slowly, it became something else.

Research on mortality salience—the psychological effects of conscious awareness of death and impermanence—has produced a surprising finding. Terror Management Theory, developed by psychologists Jeff Greenberg, Sheldon Solomon, and Tom Pyszczynski, posits that much of human behavior is driven by the anxiety of knowing we will die. But the research also shows that when people are helped to confront mortality in a psychologically integrated rather than avoidant way, it reliably produces increases in what researchers call "authentic living"—greater presence, deeper relationships, less concern with superficial status and more investment in what genuinely matters.

I began to notice, in the months after my mother died, that I was less tolerant of my own procrastination and avoidance. Less willing to stay in relationships or situations that felt hollow. More likely to say the thing that needed saying, to call the friend I had been meaning to call, to sit outside in the evening simply watching the light change because it was beautiful and would not always be available.

## The practice of impermanence as a source of joy

The Japanese aesthetic concept of *mono no aware*—sometimes translated as "the pathos of things" or "the bittersweet awareness of impermanence"—holds that beauty is inseparable from transience. The cherry blossom is most beautiful precisely because it falls. The sunset is precious because it passes. Fully experiencing the beauty of anything requires accepting that it will not last.

This is not a philosophy of despair. It is a philosophy of attention. To be fully present with what you have, while you have it, because you have genuinely accepted that you will not have it forever—this is one of the most profound shifts available to a human consciousness, and it produces something very close to what researchers mean when they measure "life satisfaction" and "well-being."

Dr. Kirk Warren Brown and Richard Ryan at Carnegie Mellon and University of Rochester conducted studies showing that mindful awareness of the present moment—attending fully to experience as it occurs rather than moving through life in a semi-distracted future-orientation—is among the most reliable predictors of emotional well-being. And the awareness of impermanence, researchers in this tradition argue, is one of the most powerful invitations to presence there is. What you know you will lose, you attend to more fully.

## What I became in the aftermath

Three years after my mother's death, I am not the same person who walked out of that meeting and sat down in a stairwell. I am less certain about many things. Less defended in ways I used to think were maturity and now recognize were armor. I have a different relationship with time—more honest about its passing, less willing to defer what matters to some safer future that never quite arrives.

I have also found a strange gratitude—tentative, sometimes painful, real. For the conversations we did have. For her laugh, which I can still hear clearly. For the ways she is still alive in how I speak and what I value and the way I make tea. The grief researcher Pauline Boss coined the term "ambiguous loss" for losses that are present even in absence—where the person is gone but not entirely gone, carried forward in those who loved them. My mother is an ambiguous loss. She is still, somehow, here.

The psychologist Viktor Frankl, who survived three years in Nazi concentration camps, wrote that "everything can be taken from a man but one thing: the last of the human freedoms—to choose one's attitude in any given set of circumstances." Suffering, he argued, becomes bearable—and sometimes even meaningful—when we discover within it a freedom that cannot be taken away.

I do not know that I would have chosen to learn what loss taught me. But I have come to believe, with some awe, that the human capacity to find meaning in difficulty, to grow through pain, to love more fully for having lost—this is not a consolation prize. It may be one of the most beautiful things about us.

## Key Concepts at a Glance

| Concept | What It Means | Why It Matters |
|---------|--------------|----------------|
| **Resilience in bereavement** | Research shows most people adapt to and find meaning after major loss, often within one to two years | Counters cultural narratives of prolonged incapacity; highlights the human capacity for metabolizing grief |
| **Post-traumatic growth** | Positive psychological changes—deepened relationships, increased strength, expanded meaning—that can emerge through adversity | Growth is not despite suffering but through it; wrestling with loss is itself the mechanism of transformation |
| **Impermanence (anicca)** | The Buddhist teaching that all phenomena are transient and that clinging to permanence causes suffering | Accepting impermanence reduces suffering and enhances presence; fully experiencing beauty requires accepting its transience |
| **Mortality salience and authentic living** | Conscious awareness of death reliably increases investment in what genuinely matters—relationships, presence, meaning | Integrated (not avoidant) awareness of impermanence produces more authentic, engaged living |
| **Mono no aware** | The Japanese aesthetic of bittersweet appreciation of impermanence; beauty inseparable from transience | A practical philosophy of attention: what you know you will lose, you attend to more fully and love more consciously |
| **Ambiguous loss** | Loss where the person is gone but still present—carried forward in memory, values, and the ways they shaped us | Reframes grief as continuation rather than only ending; allows for a more integrated relationship with loss over time |
